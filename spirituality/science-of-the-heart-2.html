The Science of the Heart:
A Complete Methodology of Sufism

Taṣawwuf is Islam's most rigorous attempt to close the gap between what a person professes and what a person becomes. This is the discipline in full: its questions, its history, its methods, its debates, and its future.

YB
Yasir Bilgin Fikir Defteri Editorial · March 8, 2026
Section I

Defining the Discipline

There is a story — debated in its details but persistent in its meaning — about Junayd of Baghdad, the ninth-century master often called the Imām of the sober path. When asked to define taṣawwuf, he did not reach for metaphors about light or love. He said: "It is that God makes you die to yourself and live through Him." The definition is spare. It is also devastating. It names the problem every sincere Muslim already knows: that ritual performance, however precise, does not automatically produce the reality it gestures toward. One can pray five times a day for forty years and remain, in the deepest sense, absent.

Sufism — taṣawwuf — is the Islamic discipline that refuses to accept this gap as permanent. It investigates why the gap exists, maps the inner terrain through which a person must travel to close it, and provides a tested methodology for doing so. Where theology (kalām) answers the question "What is God?", and law (fiqh) answers "What must I do?", taṣawwuf addresses the question that the other disciplines quietly presuppose has already been solved: "What kind of person am I becoming?"

The Classical Definition: "Taṣawwuf is the science of spiritual states and stations — of the heart's journey from heedlessness toward presence, from the love of self toward the love of God, from the performance of religion toward the reality of religion."

Section II

The Big Questions in Sufism

Every mature intellectual tradition is defined not only by its answers but by the quality of its questions. Sufism has never been intellectually monolithic. It has sustained, across fourteen centuries, a set of recurring debates that illuminate the discipline's essential tensions.

2.1Is Sufism Essential or Optional?

The question cuts to the discipline's self-understanding. If taṣawwuf is simply another name for the iḥsān dimension described in the famous Hadith of Jibrīl — "to worship God as though you see Him" — then it is not optional at all.

"The outer without the inner is hypocrisy. The inner without the outer is heresy."

Al-Ghazālī, Iḥyāʾ ʿUlūm al-Dīn
Section III

Timeline of Development

1st–2nd Century AH · 7th–8th CE
The Foundational Period: Age of the Ascetics
Early Muslims lived close to the prophetic example. Ḥasan al-Baṣrī preached the terror of divine accountability; Rābiʿa al-ʿAdawiyya shifted the register toward pure love; Ibrāhīm b. Adham modeled the renunciation of worldly status.
5th–7th Century AH · 11th–13th CE
Classical Synthesis
Al-Ghazālī achieved the tradition's greatest synthesis: his Iḥyāʾ ʿUlūm al-Dīn integrated fiqh, kalām, and taṣawwuf into a unified framework.
Section IV

Theoretical Frameworks

Qādiriyya
Founded by ʿAbd al-Qādir al-Jīlānī (d. 1166). Emphasis on public teaching and generosity.
Shādhiliyya
Founded by Abū'l-Ḥasan al-Shādhilī (d. 1258). Remembrance integrated with worldly engagement.
Naqshbandiyya
Founded by Bahāʾ al-Dīn Naqshband (d. 1389). Silent dhikr, sobriety, "solitude in the crowd."
Chishtiyya
Founded by Muʿīn al-Dīn Chishtī (d. 1236). Love, music, hospitality, service to the poor.
